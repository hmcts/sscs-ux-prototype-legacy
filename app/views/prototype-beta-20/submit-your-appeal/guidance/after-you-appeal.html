5. What happens after you’ve appealed

Your appeal is sent to the department

Your appeal is sent to the department which made the decision about your entitlement to benefits. This will either be the Department for Work and Pensions, HMRC or your local authority.

The department responds to your appeal

The department which made the decision writes a response. This should explain how they’ve made their decision and include any evidence. You can respond to this if you want.

You may be asked some additional questions

You may receive some additional questions by email or letter. These are to help the tribunal make a decision on your appeal.

Your appeal is ‘heard’

An informal hearing takes place in front of an independent tribunal. They will make an impartial decision on your entitlement to benefits.

The time it takes to appeal

It’s hard to say how long your appeal will take. It all depends on how busy the hearing venue is and how many appeals are being made. They can take several months though. You can avoid delays by:

  • sending any evidence well before the hearing (if you have any)
  • turning up to the hearing on time (if you choose to go)
  • getting a representative early (if you need one)

Sending evidence to support your appeal

Evidence can help the tribunal understand your condition or circumstances. It could be a letter from a doctor or a written statement which explains your side of the appeal.

It’s important you send in evidence as soon as possible after you’ve submitted your appeal. This is so the tribunal has time to read it before your hearing. You’ll be sent more information on how to send in evidence after you’ve submitted your appeal.
