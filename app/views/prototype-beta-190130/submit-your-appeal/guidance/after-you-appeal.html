What happens after you’ve appealed

Your appeal is sent to the department

Your appeal is sent to the department which made the decision about your entitlement to benefits. This will either be the Department for Work and Pensions, HMRC or your local authority.

The department responds to your appeal

The department which made the decision writes a response. This should explain how they’ve made their decision and include any evidence. You can respond to this if you want.

Your appeal is ‘heard’

A hearing takes place in front of an independent panel of people, known as a tribunal. They are completely separate to the department which made the original decision.

The time it takes to appeal

It’s hard to say how long it will take to get a decision on your appeal. It depends on how busy the hearing venue is and how many appeals are being made. They can take several months though.

You can avoid delays by:

  • sending any evidence well before the hearing (if you have any)
  • turning up to the hearing on time (if you choose to go)
  • getting a representative early (if you need one)

Sending evidence to support your appeal

Evidence can help the tribunal understand your condition or circumstances. For example, a letter from a doctor or a written statement by someone who knows you. Different evidence is useful depending on the benefit you are appealing. There is more specific guidance in the service.

For some benefit appeals you can upload evidence when are submitting the appeal. You can also post it in. You should send it as soon as possible if you are posting it. This is so the tribunal have time to read it before your hearing. You’ll be sent more information on how to send in evidence after you’ve submitted your appeal.
